Anaximenes’ Philosophy of Air and the Secret of Motion

Introduction

Following Thales’ Universalism and Anaximander’s Apeironism, we arrive at the third and final thinker of the Milesian School: Anaximenes. His system introduces a novel understanding of the cosmos, where existence is governed by the transformation of a single, all-encompassing substance: air (pneuma).

Unlike his predecessors, Anaximenes did not simply propose another element as the fundamental principle. Instead, he provided a structured explanation of how different material forms arise through the compression and rarefaction of air. This process of condensation (substantialization) and rarefaction (de-substantialization) underlies the dynamic nature of reality.

The Field Theory: Universal vs. Global

At the heart of Anaximenes’ thought is the distinction between two fundamental fields:

  • Universal – The rational field, where order is continuously imposed through symbolic structures.
  • Global – The self-contained, self-circulating field, where internal contradictions emerge.

In simple terms, the Universal is an abstracted field of reason that imposes logical structure upon the world, while the Global is the totality of existence, containing all contradictions and chaotic transformations.

This distinction is crucial to understanding how symbolic systems interact with reality. The universalized rationality, built upon logic and structured thought, overlays the global field of existence like an imposed order. Just as in global capitalism, where structural contradictions inevitably generate crises (such as class struggle), Anaximenes’ framework acknowledges the tension between imposed rationality and organic existence.

Ontology: Pneuma as the Fundamental Substance

Whereas Thales posited water as the primary element and Anaximander introduced Apeiron as a formless principle, Anaximenes settled on pneuma—air or breath—as the true origin of all things.

Pneuma (πνεῦμα) is a substance that is at once formless and capable of transformation. Everything in the universe, from fire to stone, is merely a different state of condensed or rarefied air.

Epistemology: Rational Understanding of Natural Phenomena

Anaximenes’ philosophy was deeply concerned with how we perceive and understand the world. Unlike the mystical elements of earlier cosmologies, his system was built upon observable processes.

By analyzing the transitions between different states of matter, he proposed a structured model of reality where natural phenomena follow logical laws. This approach made his philosophy one of the earliest forms of scientific materialism.

Teleology: The Dynamics of Rarefaction and Condensation

The core mechanism of Anaximenes’ system is the dual movement of:

  • Rarefaction – The process of becoming lighter, more diffuse, and immaterial.
  • Condensation – The process of becoming heavier, more substantial, and material.

He illustrated this transformation through natural examples: Air becomes wind, wind becomes clouds, clouds condense into water, water solidifies into earth, and earth into stone. The reverse process—heating and expanding—transforms solid matter back into lighter states, eventually returning to pure air.

The Philosophical Felting Technique

To truly grasp Anaximenes’ philosophy, we must understand the hidden metaphor within his system: felting.

Felting is the process of turning raw wool into fabric through compression, heating, and interweaving. In the same way, the universe is structured through a continuous process of substantialization and de-substantialization. This is why I call his system a kind of "philosophical felting technique."

Just as different elements—fire, wind, water, and earth—play a role in felting, they also participate in the formation of material reality. Each stage of condensation and rarefaction is akin to a different stage of felting: heating, pressing, soaking, and drying.

Application: Understanding Rainbows

One of the most fascinating aspects of Anaximenes’ philosophy is his explanation of rainbows. He believed that when sunlight (fire) interacts with wind, it applies a condensation force, extracting a hidden structured pattern within the air, revealing different colors.

His explanation, though pre-scientific, aligns with modern ideas of refraction, where light interacts with particles to produce structured visible effects.
